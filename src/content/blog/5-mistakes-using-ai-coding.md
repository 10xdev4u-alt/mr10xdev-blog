After 6 months of daily AI use: 5 mistakes I made. The
over-reliance, the under-review, the wrong tool for the job, the
missing context, the lost skill. The honest assessment.

## Mistake 1: Over-reliance

I started using AI for everything. Every line of code, every
commit message, every doc. I let AI do the thinking. I
reviewed the output but didn't think about whether it was
right.

**The cost:** my skills atrophied. I couldn't write a 50-line
function without AI. I couldn't think about a design without
AI. I lost the muscle.

**The fix:** use AI for the 80% (boilerplate, tests, docs).
Use my brain for the 20% (architecture, design, key
decisions). Keep the skills sharp by doing the 20% by hand.

## Mistake 2: Under-review

I trusted AI output. I assumed it was right. I didn't run the
code. I didn't read the docs. I shipped bugs.

**The cost:** 2 critical bugs in production. 1 security issue.
1 user data leak (caught before the data went out).

**The fix:** always review. Always run the code. Always read
the docs. AI is a tool, not a replacement for thinking.

## Mistake 3: Wrong tool for the job

I used AI for things AI isn't good at. Architecture. Design.
Key decisions. I asked AI to design the system, and it gave
me 5 mediocre options.

**The cost:** I shipped a mediocre system. The system was
hard to change. The system was hard to test. The system was
hard to understand.

**The fix:** AI is great for the 80% (mechanical). AI is
terrible for the 20% (creative). Use the right tool for the
right job. AI is a tool, not a designer.

## Mistake 4: Missing context

I asked AI to do things without giving it the context. "Add a
test for this function." But the AI didn't know what the
function did. The AI wrote a test that didn't test anything.

**The cost:** I shipped 50 tests that didn't test the right
thing. The tests passed but didn't catch any bugs.

**The fix:** give AI the context. Show the function. Show the
manifest. Show the example. The more context, the better the
output.

## Mistake 5: Lost skill

After 6 months of daily AI use, I couldn't write a 100-line
function without AI. I couldn't think about a design without
AI. I lost the muscle.

**The cost:** I became dependent. I couldn't work without AI.
I couldn't review AI output without a lot of effort. I
couldn't debug AI output without understanding the code.

**The fix:** keep the skills sharp. Do the 20% by hand. Use
AI for the 80%. Don't let AI do the thinking.

## The 5 lessons

### Lesson 1: Use AI for the 80%, not the 100%

AI is great for the mechanical work. AI is bad for the
creative work. Use the right tool for the right job.

### Lesson 2: Always review the output

Never trust AI output. Always run the code. Always read the
docs. AI is a tool, not a replacement for thinking.

### Lesson 3: Be specific

The more specific the prompt, the better the output. "Make
this better" gives bad output. "Refactor this 500-line file"
gives good output.

### Lesson 4: Give AI the context

AI doesn't know what you know. Give it the function, the
manifest, the example. The more context, the better the
output.

### Lesson 5: Keep your skills sharp

Don't let AI do the thinking. Do the 20% by hand. Use AI
for the 80%. Keep the muscle.

## The honest balance

AI helps me 4x. AI hurts me 1x. Net: 4x faster.

The savings:
- 5-7 days of manual work
- ~5 hours of debugging
- ~2 hours of documentation
- ~2 hours of test writing

The cost:
- ~2 hours of refactoring over-abstraction
- ~1 hour of debugging hallucinated APIs
- ~1 hour of re-prompting context overflow

Net savings: ~12 days. The 12 days were spent on more
features, more tests, more docs. The compound effect is real.

## The meta-lesson

AI is the most powerful tool I've ever used. It's also the
most dangerous. The power is the leverage. The danger is the
loss of skill.

The agent that uses AI well is 10x. The agent that uses AI
poorly is 1x (or worse). The choice is yours.

The agent era is here. The AI is the tool. The tool is the
leverage. The leverage compounds. The discipline is the
balance.
